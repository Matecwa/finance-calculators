What is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple screening tool that estimates body fat based on your height and weight. It was developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s and is widely used by healthcare professionals as a quick assessment of weight status. BMI applies to adult men and women and is calculated differently for children.

How BMI is Calculated

The BMI formula divides your weight by the square of your height. In metric units: BMI = weight (kg) / height (m)². In imperial units: BMI = (weight (lbs) × 703) / height (inches)². For example, a person who weighs 160 lbs and is 5'9" tall has a BMI of 23.6, which falls in the normal weight category.

BMI Categories

The World Health Organization defines four main categories: Underweight (BMI below 18.5), Normal weight (BMI 18.5 to 24.9), Overweight (BMI 25 to 29.9), and Obese (BMI 30 and above). These ranges are based on population studies linking BMI to health outcomes.

Limitations of BMI

BMI does not directly measure body fat and has important limitations. It may overestimate body fat in athletes and muscular individuals, and underestimate it in older adults who have lost muscle mass. BMI also does not account for fat distribution — abdominal fat carries greater health risks than fat stored in other areas. For a complete health picture, consult with a healthcare professional who can consider additional factors.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is BMI accurate for athletes?

BMI can be misleading for athletes and very muscular people. Muscle weighs more than fat, so a fit athlete might have a "overweight" BMI despite having low body fat. Waist circumference or body fat percentage measurements may be more useful in these cases.

What is a healthy BMI?

A BMI between 18.5 and 24.9 is generally considered healthy for adults. However, "healthy" depends on many factors beyond BMI, including muscle mass, bone density, age, sex, and overall fitness level.

Does BMI differ by age?

For adults 20 and older, BMI categories are the same regardless of age. For children and teens (ages 2-19), BMI is interpreted using age- and sex-specific percentile charts because body composition changes as children grow.
